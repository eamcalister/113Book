The ideal gas law says that
\begin{equation*} PV = nR(T-z), \end{equation*}
where \(P\) is the pressure of a gas, \(V\) is its volume, \(T\) is its temperature in \(^{\circ}\)C, \(n\) is the number of moles of gas, \(R\) is a constant, and \(z\) is the value of absolute zero in \(^{\circ}\)C. If \(n\) and \(V\) are constant, like when the gas is in a sealed container, we have a linear function for \(P\) as a function of \(T\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} P = \frac{nR}{V} (T-z). \end{equation*}
  1. What is the slope of the graph of \(P\) (just in symbols, not the number)?
  2. Suppose we measure \(P\) to be \(3.5\) when \(T = 70\text{,}\) and \(3.602\) when \(T = 80\text{.}\) Find the slope of the graph of \(P\) from this data.
  3. Use the data and the value of the slope from part (b) to give an approximate value of \(z\text{.}\) Is this close to the value you may have learned in a science class (or can look up)?
